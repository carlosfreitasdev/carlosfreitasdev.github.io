12 Rules for Life: An Antidote to Chaos

12 Rules for Life: An Antidote to Chaos is a self-help book written by Canadian clinical psychologist Jordan Peterson. The book offers life tips through essays on ethical principles, psychology, mythology, religion and personal anecdotes.

In this summary I would like to briefly introduce you to the 12 rules provided in the book.

1. "Stand up straight with your shoulders back"

Humans have a dominance detector in their brains. The way we perceive our socio-economic status affects our well-being, which reinforces our status in a positive feedback loop.

  • People with high self-esteem feel/transmit a sense of security and confidence, which makes them more attractive and respected. This improves their productivity and well-being, which further strengthens their self-perception. Higher serotonin levels are associated with greater resilience, happiness, health, life expectancy, pro-social behavior and leadership.
  • On the other hand, people with a low self-perception feel insecure. They are more likely to be nervous and reactive. They make bad decisions and fail to command respect or resources, which reinforces their failure complex.

In short, our self-perception affects the vibrations we transmit, creating a cycle that reinforces itself. If, for some reason, you feel like a failure, the first step is to break the negative cycle by correcting your posture.

  • Don’t slouch. It conveys defeat and a low status, which prompts others to treat you poorly and reinforce your low self-perception.
  • Fix your posture. Stand straight, push your shoulders back, speak up and make eye contact. This signals confidence to yourself and others. You’ll feel better, others will show you more respect, and it starts a virtuous cycle. It’s about standing tall, facing up to reality and taking responsibility to become all you can be.

2. "Treat yourself like someone you are responsible for helping"

We're better at looking after others than ourselves. This is because we're often hyper-aware of our own shortcomings and this awareness can lead us to feel self-loathing. As we dwell on all the reasons why we consider ourselves inferior, we convince ourselves that we don't deserve personal care, kindness or positivity. As a result, we tend to focus all our positive energy on those we love, especially our partners and pets, and stop giving ourselves the same kindness.

Eastern philosophy recognizes these toxic patterns through its teachings on the duality of nature. The Yin-Yang symbol represents a dark and a light side, and implies that each side contains at least a hint of the other and that neither can exist without the other. Eastern philosophy also posits that harmony can only be achieved by embracing both and seeking a healthy balance between the two. This balance can be achieved by avoiding extremes.

  • Treat yourself like someone you are responsible for helping. Consider what would be truly good for you. This isn't "What do I want?" or "What would make me happy?".
  • Consider the future and think: "What would my life be like if I took proper care of myself?".

3. "Make friends with people who want the best for you"

When well-meaning teachers place a delinquent teenager among comparatively civilized peers, delinquency spreads, not stability. It's much easier to go down than up.

Furthermore, if we believe the story that everything terrible happened by itself, without any personal responsibility on the part of the victim, we deny that person all power in the past (and, implicitly, also in the present and future). In this way, we take away all their power.

Here's something to consider: If you have a friend whose friendship you wouldn't recommend to your sister, your father or your son, why would you have that friend for yourself?

  • Choose people who want things to be better, not worse. It's a good, unselfish thing to choose people who are good to you. It's appropriate and commendable to associate with people whose lives would improve if they saw your life improve.
  • Don't think that it's easier to surround yourself with good, healthy people than with bad, unhealthy people. A good, healthy person is an ideal. It takes strength and bravery to get close to such person. Have a little humility and courage. Use your judgment and protect yourself from overly uncritical compassion and pity.

4. "Compare yourself to who you were yesterday, not to who someone else is today"

How often do you find yourself comparing your success with that of others? Whether it's with the intention of lifting yourself up with statements like: "Well, at least I did better than him/her!" or to discourage yourself with comments like: "I'll never win that award like him/her", this pattern of thinking is very toxic. This is because comparison is a progress killer. As such, it's important to clarify here that comparison and self-criticism are not the same thing. Self-criticism is healthy (up to a point) because it invites you to make a realistic assessment of your shortcomings and identify areas where you can improve. This is what motivates us to work towards a brighter and more successful future.

So stop comparing yourself to others and instead judge yourself based on your own previous achievements. Are you better today than you were yesterday? Do you want to be better tomorrow than you were today? If the answer is yes (and it should be), then this is the secret to staying on track. Because when you compare your present with your past, you recognize that you are growing in the way that suits you and at the speed that suits you. You'll be able to recognize the small moments of growth that are critical in developing your best self and you'll be able to appreciate the unique facets of your life that make your progress specific to you.

  • Pay attention. Focus on your environment, both physical and psychological. Notice something that bothers you, that worries you, that doesn't let you be, that you could solve and that you would solve.
  • Ask yourself (as if you really wanted to know): "What's bothering me?" "Can I solve it?" and "Am I really willing to solve it?" If the answer is "no" to any or all of the questions, then look for something else. Aim lower. Look until you find something that bothers you, that you can solve, that you want to solve, and then solve it. That might be enough for the day.

5. "Do not let your children do anything that makes you dislike them"

The right way to educate our children is one of life's greatest dilemmas and torments many struggling parents. Since our children come into the world as blank slates, we are often paralyzed by the question of what to write in people who will have an impact on future generations. Niebauer suggests that the starting point for answering this question is to recognize innate human aggression. We all know how nasty children can be towards each other; almost everyone has a history of bullying, at least at some point in their childhood. So what if our main concern was simply to raise kind children? Niebauer says that cultivating kindness requires more than being friends with your child; in fact, being a successful parent requires taking the risk that there will be times when your child doesn't like you.

Although it can be a bit painful, take a moment to think about how this style of parenting would work. After all, if you're focused on being your child's best friend, you're not focused on imposing rules; instead, your job is to make sure they're always happy and having fun. And that's not going to help your child become a well-adjusted or likeable adult. So while it may not be fun to set limits and enforce them, it can be useful to remember that you're doing your child a great service by teaching them life lessons from an early age. After all, isn't it better for them to learn responsibility from a loving parent than from a society that will teach them in much less gentle ways? So if you're eager to help your child become the best they can be, here are Niebauer's top tips for successful parenting.

  • Avoid too many rules. Although rules are absolutely necessary, if you impose too many, your child will become frustrated and feel as if they are surrounded by obstacles. So instead of controlling their lives down to the minutiae with rules like "Your socks always have to be the same" or "You always have to be in bed by this time", focus on practical, real rules, like showing respect, being kind and never using violence, except in self-defense. Remember that these rules will help shape your child's core values and, honestly, would you rather have a child who is caring and respectful or a little monster whose socks always match?
  • Always use the least amount of force necessary. Make sure you set clear rules with equally clear consequences and take care to match the punishment to the crime. Knowing which punishments are most effective in helping your child learn the consequences of their actions also requires a deep knowledge of your child, so get to know the unique person they are and understand when only verbal correction is needed and when you should take away video games for a week.
  • Stand as a united front. If the household consists of two parents, make sure you tell your child that they can't turn you against each other to get what they want. Similarly, don't forget to support your partner, even when they make mistakes. Recognizing mistakes with love and working together to correct them is the key to growth and a happy home.
  • Understand your own capacity to be harsh, vindictive, arrogant, resentful and deceitful. People are aggressive and selfish, as well as kind and caring. For this reason, no adult human being can really tolerate being dominated by a child. Revenge will come. Ten minutes after a pair of overly kind and patient parents have failed to prevent a public tantrum in the supermarket, they will give their child a cold stare when he comes running excitedly to show his mother and father his latest conquest.

6. "Set your house in perfect order before you criticize the world"

"Why do bad things happen to good people?" is one of life's oldest and most distressing mysteries and, to this day, we still haven't solved it. Often, this is because it's easier to blame others for injustice, whether on a global or personal scale. However, this is neither the correct response to the world's suffering, nor our temptation to give in to despair. Although both reactions are tempting, avoiding extreme reactions is crucial for a healthy lifestyle.

Drawing on the philosophy of Russian writer Aleksandr Solzenhitsyn, Peterson argues that it is possible to reject the cruelty of life even when you see it first hand. This is what Solzenhitsyn learned when, after a dedicated fight against the Nazis during the Second World War, he was imprisoned by his own state after the war ended. You might think that life in a Russian gulag would be as bad as it gets, but for Solzenhitsyn it was even worse: while serving his sentence, he discovered that he had cancer.

However, despite everything, Solzenhitsyn resisted the temptation to become spiteful. Preferring to focus on the good side of humanity, he spent his time in prison looking for opportunities to contribute something meaningful in the time he had left. That mission included authoring the book The Gulag Archipelago, a scathing exposé of the Soviet internment camps. This book not only gave Solzenhitsyn an additional purpose in life, but also played a vital role in demolishing public support for Stalin's communism.

  • Get your life together. Start stopping doing what you know is wrong. Start stopping today. Don't waste time wondering how you know what you're doing is wrong if you're sure it is.
  • Have some humility. Don't reorganize a group until you've sorted out your own experience. If you can't bring peace to your home, how dare you try to rule a city?

7. "Pursue what is meaningful (not what is expedient)"

"No tree can grow to Heaven unless it’s roots reach down to Hell." - Carl Gustav Jung

Have you ever heard the story of the monkey who got his hand stuck in the cookie jar? Although his hand fit inside the jar, it was too big for him to get it out while holding a cookie. The story ends with the monkey being captured by hunters, with the moral suggesting that greed is a trap that holds us captive. You've probably witnessed similar behavior in human beings; the temptation to pursue what we want, even when it leads to negative consequences, is a fairly universal human experience. But giving in to that desire doesn't make us better people, does it?

If we see the world as bleak and miserable, we naturally seek to relieve our depression by pursuing any pleasure that will make our existence more bearable. This often leads us to justify that something can't be wrong if it makes us happy, and that kind of rationalization can be a gateway to very bad decisions. So how can we combat this? Peterson suggests that our best recourse is to seek sacrifice rather than instant gratification. This is because sacrifice puts better things in the future by giving up instant pleasure in the present. And, in a way, we already know this; after all, we sacrifice things we'd rather be doing in order to go to work every day and earn a living.

But that kind of sacrifice is still in the interest of personal gain, because we do it to facilitate our own survival or to save up for the promise of a vacation later on. However, Peterson argues that the real sacrifice - the kind of sacrifice we need to make to become kinder, happier and better adjusted people - is defined by the things we do for others. So instead of looking for instant, selfish pleasure, consider dedicating some of your time to volunteering and making a difference in someone else's life. When you focus on the good you can do in the world, you take a break from your negative view of the world and let yourself be guided by positive influences.

  • Having meaning in life is better than having what you want, because you may not know what you want or what you really need. Meaning is something that comes to us of its own accord.
  • What's convenient only works for the moment. It's immediate, impulsive and limited. What is meaningful, on the other hand, is the organization of what would otherwise be merely convenient in a symphony of Being.

8. "Tell the truth – or, at least, don't lie"

We all tend to lie to ourselves in one way or another. Whether it's telling ourselves that we're going to achieve a certain goal without putting in the effort, or deluding ourselves about our shortcomings, we're all liars. The Austrian psychologist Alfred Adler called this habit "life lies" and defined them as "the things we say and do to get what we think we want". This "what we think we want" distinction is important because it recognizes the fact that we often deceive ourselves into wanting things that, in the end, are not good for us. The same applies to our ability to convince ourselves that we already know everything we need to know.

This is an especially insidious lie, because if we assume that we already have the right tools for self-improvement, we lose all will to learn and grow. That's why Peterson's eighth rule of life is: stop lying! Perfect your self-awareness and learn to recognize when you're telling yourself one of life's lies. And then work to correct it. Realigning your goals and your personal truth can help you get your life on track and be honest about your progress.

9. "Assume that the person you are listening to might know something you don't"

If you spend any time on social media, you know that it can be a hotbed of conflict and scathing criticism. People often take offense too quickly and lash out, spewing hateful things at random strangers on the Internet. Sadly, this behavior has come to characterize the state of conversation in our society and is a total perversion of what healthy debate used to mean. To regain your ability to engage in genuine conversation, it is necessary to return to the truths of the ancient Greek philosophers who understood that respectfully challenging someone's idea is not the same thing as attacking them.

Following this method, Peterson applies his ninth life lesson, which consists of listening to what others have to say and assuming that we have something to learn from them. Instead of treating the conversation as a competition, looking for opportunities to prove that we're right and the other person is wrong, or looking for reasons to be offended, simply focus on listening. You may disagree with someone, and that's okay; even in those cases, you should approach every conversation following the simple method of listening and then summarizing to recap what you've heard out loud. Rephrasing what you've understood in your own words is an excellent way of communicating with the other person and ensuring that you've heard them correctly. It also shows that you care enough to listen and assimilate what they say.

10. "Be precise In Your Speech"

It's no secret that life can be confusing and that our relationships with others can sometimes be even more complicated. However, we can alleviate some of this confusion by recognizing that sometimes the reason we can't understand things is because we only pay attention to the details that interest us or make sense to us. And although this is a natural human inclination - after all, we're not physically capable of thinking about everything all the time! - our inability to see the bigger picture can often make the world seem even more chaotic. That's why we need rule ten: use precise language.

Confronting the complexities of the world with the clarity of precise language is invaluable, because it helps us to break down complex concepts into small, simple pieces of information that we can understand. By simplifying the situation through precise terminology, we can establish order in our lives. Whether the problem is a car breakdown or an illness in the body, by specifically articulating the symptoms, we can begin to regain control. The same applies to conflicts in relationships. If your feelings are hurt or you're trying to solve a problem with your partner, precise language can help you articulate the problem simply and honestly.

11. "Do not bother children while they are skateboarding"

As today's society tries to correct a culture of injustice, it can be difficult to know where the boundaries are. Our efforts to combat the evils of toxic masculinity sometimes lead people to stigmatize men and masculinity. And that shouldn't be the answer! While it is extremely true that men have behaved deplorably for centuries, we should not unfairly direct our sense of outrage at all men. This view of the world leads to destruction rather than solving a problem or creating a solution, and this should not be the goal of social justice. Instead, we should focus on blending our differences to achieve a harmonious future.

Because many men, just like women, are aggressive or dominant by nature and these traits don't have to be a bad thing when they are channeled in the right direction. With encouragement and guidance on how to avoid becoming part of the problem, men can channel these personality traits to accomplish incredible things that contribute to the greater good of society. And to illustrate this point, Peterson considers the example of skateboarders. One day, he noticed that, on the campus of the University of Toronto, some young skateboarders were demonstrating incredible feats of agility and balance. However, as social norms often classify skateboarding as undesirable or inappropriate, city officials decided to ban skateboarding on campus.

This policy failed to take into account teenagers' dedication, skill and willingness to take physical risks and instead criminalized their behaviour. Peterson argues that this was a mistake and suggested that the same applies to any case where we slander a particular group of people. Instead of holistically stigmatizing a group we should be willing to accept new perspectives and talents that differ from our own. Ultimately, this is the worldview that should guide our society, because it invites us to create rules that protect us without suppressing the good qualities that different people can bring to the table. So whether it's skaters or persistent, aggressive men, we shouldn't criminalize the behaviour of others unless it's actively harming someone else, because then it really is part of the problem.

12. "Pet a cat when you encounter one in the street"

We've already touched on the fact that life is often full of sadness, but it's important to remember that life is also full of joy. The main difference is that, while sadness often threatens to overwhelm us, slapping us in the face, we have to look a little harder to find joy. Peterson knows this first-hand, because her daughter has been battling severe arthritis since she was six years old. This degenerative disease filled her childhood with extreme pain, frequent injections and multiple invasive surgeries to replace entire joints. If you're reading this and you think life is unfair, you're right! There's nothing fair about an innocent child suffering so much pain. But Peterson says that the darkness of these moments should only reinforce our motivation to look for the good in life.

That's why rule number twelve is: make the most of even the smallest joys that life offers. In his own experience, Peterson has discovered that relentless positivity is the only way to sustain yourself even in the darkest moments. Because, although he is deeply affected by his daughter's suffering, he is grateful that, after years of pain, she has finally found a physiotherapist who can help her achieve greater mobility and develop a life with much more normality and much less pain. Despite knowing that she's not completely out of the woods yet and that more complications could arise in the future, Peterson is grateful for the small mercies in her life and tries to keep them positive for both of them. To this end, he advises readers to always avoid sadness and to be aware of even the smallest things that can make you happy, like petting a cat you meet on a walk.
